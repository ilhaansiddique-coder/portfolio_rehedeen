## Why CPSIA Matters for Importers

The **Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act (CPSIA)** places legal responsibility on the **importer of record** — not the factory. If your brand's name is on the import documents, you are responsible for ensuring the products comply, that you have documentation proving it, and that the documentation is available to CPSC inspectors on demand.

A CPSIA violation can mean a mandatory recall, civil penalties up to $100,000 per violation, and criminal liability for executives in serious cases. This is not paperwork bureaucracy — it's a strict liability regime.

## The Core Requirements for Children's Apparel

### 1. 3rd-Party Testing

Children's products (designed for children 12 years and under) **must be tested by an accredited 3rd-party laboratory** before the first commercial shipment and annually thereafter (or when there is a material change in the product or production process).

What to test:
- **Lead content** (substrate and surface coating — separate tests)
- **Phthalates** (6 restricted types — DEHP, DBP, BBP, DINP, DIDP, DnOP)
- **Flammability** (see Section 4 below)

**Accepted labs:** CPSC maintains a list of accredited labs. Use SGS, Bureau Veritas, Intertek, or QIMA — all are accredited and have Bangladesh operations. Avoid labs you've not heard of; if they're not on the CPSC acceptance list, the tests don't count.

### 2. General Conformity Certificate (GCC)

A GCC is a document certifying that your product complies with applicable CPSC regulations. The importer (not the factory) is technically the issuer, but in practice, factories produce GCCs and importers countersign.

**A GCC must state:**
- The product it covers (by style number and description)
- The specific regulations it complies with (e.g., 16 CFR 1501, CPSIA Section 101)
- The 3rd-party test reports supporting each claim
- Contact information for the issuer

One GCC per style is the standard practice. If a style has significant construction or material variation between colourways, separate GCCs may be required.

### 3. Tracking Labels

Every children's product must carry a **permanent tracking label** on the product itself (not just on the packaging). For garments, this is a sewn label or heat transfer.

**Required tracking label content:**
- Manufacturer name and location
- Date of manufacture (month and year minimum)
- Lot/batch number or other specific identification
- Product identifier that allows the item to be traced back to the specific production run

This label must be distinct from the care label — it carries specific identity information, not just care instructions.

### 4. Flammability for Sleepwear

Children's sleepwear is uniquely regulated under **16 CFR 1615** (sizes 0–6X) and **16 CFR 1616** (sizes 7–14). There are two compliance paths:

**Path A — Snug-fitting sleepwear:** Garments that are snug-fitting (defined by specific dimensions relative to body measurements) may be exempt from flame-resistance treatment. Label must state "Wear snug-fitting. Not flame resistant."

**Path B — Flame-resistant fabric:** Garment fabric passes a 3-second vertical flame test per the CFR. Requires washing instruction compliance testing to ensure the flame resistance is maintained through the labelled number of washes.

Most Bangladesh manufacturers handle snug-fitting sleepwear for US buyers — it's the lower-risk, no-chemicals path.

### 5. What Changes Testing Requirements

Testing is not a once-and-done exercise. You must retest (or obtain a new test report from your factory) when:

- You change fabric supplier, dye lot, or fibre composition
- You add new trims (snaps, buttons, zippers) with a different material or coating
- You change your print ink supplier or formulation
- Production moves to a different factory or facility

## The Documentation Pack You Need Before Shipment

At minimum, before your first USA-destined container, have:

- [ ] GCC per style, signed and dated
- [ ] 3rd-party lab test reports covering lead, phthalates, and flammability
- [ ] Tracking label confirmed on all garments (verify on sealed sample)
- [ ] Care label reviewed against 16 CFR 423 (US Federal Care Labeling Rule)
- [ ] Bill of materials listing all components by CAS number for restricted substances

## Working With Your Factory to Get This Right

Tell your factory you need CPSIA documentation at the order stage, not two weeks before shipment. The test booking, scheduling, and report turnaround takes 2–3 weeks. If your factory is used to EU buyers, they may not have CPSIA documentation as a standard offering — brief them explicitly.
